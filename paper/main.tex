\begin{abstract}
Short-term load forecasting (STLF) is foundational to reliable and economical power-system
operation. Recent effort has been spent in improving forecasting models by scaling them with new large-scale pretraining dataset.  BuildingsBench 900K is one of the contributor consisting of 900{,}000 simulated buildings together with seven
real-world datasets. Its public leaderboard, however, fixes a particular model
family, while the time-series forecasting literature has since converged on a
different set of standard architectures that have not yet been evaluated. Furthermore, the original paper has not scaled models with weather data. 
We re-implement BuildingsBench's exact preprocessing, temporal
validation split, evaluation metrics, gather the relevant weather features and benchmark a broad set of
 published forecasting models: PatchTST, iTransformer, TimeXer, Informer,
Crossformer, and DLinear, alongside recurrent (LSTM, GRU) and gradient-boosted
(XGBoost, LightGBM) baselines 
Two results
follow. First, on the seven real-world datasets (1{,}923 buildings, zero-shot), the
strongest model (TimeXer, $12.57\%$ commercial NRMSE) surpasses the value reported
for the benchmark's largest pretrained transformer. Second,
weather inputs yield large gains up to $-21\%$ NRMSE for
Crossformer on the simulated, however, it does not transfer to real buildings remaining almost unchanged
(mean commercial NRMSE change $+0.05$). This exposes a concrete simulated-to-real
gap in learned weather sensitivity that a benchmark reporting only
weather ablations would miss.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Accurate short-term load forecasting (STLF) is crucial to power-system scheduling,
demand response, together with the rise of intermittent energy sources. As
demand-side behavior diversifies, forecasting increasingly relies not only on
historical load but also on exogenous drivers such as weather and calendar
effects \citep{buildingsbench2023}. Deep learning now dominates the task, yet
progress has been fragmented across different datasets and inconsistent
evaluation protocols, which makes cross-study comparison difficult.

BuildingsBench \citep{buildingsbench2023} addressed this fragmentation by releasing
Buildings-900K a corpus of roughly 900{,}000 simulated buildings for
pretraining together with a standardized zero-shot evaluation over seven
real-world and a simulated dataset. Its headline
models are Transformers with a Gaussian or tokenized output head.
Since its release, the general time-series forecasting community has
converged on a different set of standard architectures PatchTST, iTransformer,
TimeXer, and others that have never been evaluated under the BuildingsBench benchmark. Two questions therefore remain open: how do these mordern 
forecasters perform on this benchmark relative to persistence and to the
benchmark's own model family, and how much do the benchmark's weather inputs
actually contribute, in-distribution and under the zero-shot shift to real
buildings that the benchmark is built to measure?

We answer both questions and, in doing so, surface a finding that the benchmark's
own protocol cannot: the weather sensitivity that models learn on simulated data
does not transfer to real buildings. 


\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{The BuildingsBench Benchmark}
\label{sec:related-bb}
BuildingsBench \citep{buildingsbench2023} comprises Buildings-900K, a pretraining
corpus of simulated hourly load generated from the U.S.\ Department of Energy's
ResStock and ComStock building-stock models, and a zero-shot evaluation suite of
seven real building datasets. The benchmark fixes a 168-hour context and 24-hour
forecast horizon, a global Box--Cox load transform, per-channel weather
standardization, and reports NRMSE (coefficient of variation of RMSE) and a ranked
probability score (RPS), aggregated as the median across per-building values with
bootstrapped confidence intervals. Its published models are Transformer-\{S,M,L\}
variants with either a Gaussian or a tokenized output head. We adopt its
preprocessing and metrics verbatim (Section~\ref{sec:method}) so that our numbers
are directly comparable to its leaderboard.

\subsection{Deep Forecasting Architectures}
\label{sec:related-arch}
We benchmark architectures spanning the main design families of modern time-series
forecasting. \textbf{Patch-based transformers} such as PatchTST \citep{patchtst}
segment a series into subseries patches processed channel-independently.
\textbf{Inverted attention} (iTransformer, \citealp{itransformer}) treats each
variate as a token and attends across variables. \textbf{Exogenous-aware
transformers} such as TimeXer \citep{timexer} explicitly separate endogenous patch
tokens from exogenous variate tokens, a design intended for exactly the
weather-conditioned setting studied here. \textbf{Efficiency-oriented} designs
include Informer's ProbSparse attention \citep{informer} and Crossformer's
two-stage cross-dimension attention \citep{crossformer}. As a linear control we
include DLinear \citep{dlinear}, which questioned whether transformers are
necessary at all. We retain recurrent (LSTM, GRU) and gradient-boosted tree
(XGBoost, LightGBM) baselines, which remain competitive in operational STLF. Each
transformer is scaled to a common $\geq7$M-parameter budget so that architectural
differences, not capacity, drive the comparison.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Dataset and Test Sets}
\label{sec:dataset}
We train on a subsample of $N=20{,}000$ buildings from Buildings-900K, drawn from
the official weekly-sampled index file (the smallest official index that covers
$20{,}000$ buildings holds $10^{5}$). This size is chose because of the original
benchmark's own scaling analysis, which finds that zero-shot commercial performance
improves with the number of pretraining buildings as a \emph{power law with
diminishing returns}, the bulk of the gain accruing within roughly the first
$10^{3}$ buildings \citep{buildingsbench2023}. Our $20{,}000$-building sample
therefore sits an order of magnitude beyond that knee capturing most of the
attainable pretraining benefit while remaining trainable for every architecture
within a single-GPU (Colab) budget. We evaluate on two held-out sets: (i) the official \emph{Buildings-900K-test} simulated split
($N=2{,}928$ buildings), and (ii) a real-world zero-shot set of $1{,}923$ buildings
($970$ commercial, $953$ residential) pooled from seven open datasets (BDG-2,
Borealis, Electricity, IDEAL, LCL, SMART, Sceaux). Multi-year files of the same
physical building are concatenated into one continuous series.
Figure~\ref{fig:eda} characterizes the data: the load
heatmaps show the strong diurnal and seasonal structure forecasting must capture
and the differing commercial/residential profiles, and the weather--load
correlation panel motivates the weather-value analysis of Section~\ref{sec:weather}.

\subsection{Data Preprocessing}
\label{sec:preprocessing}
We use the benchmark's own already-fit transforms rather than re-fitting them: a
global Box--Cox transform on load and a per-channel standardization of the seven
official weather channels (temperature, humidity, wind speed, wind direction, and
global horizontal, direct normal, and diffuse horizontal radiation). Both are
downloaded from the benchmark's public artifact bucket and validated at load time
by a numeric round-trip check. The load transform is the Box--Cox power transform
\citep{boxcox1964}
\begin{equation}
T_{\lambda}(y)=
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{(y+\epsilon)^{\lambda}-1}{\lambda}, & \lambda\neq 0,\\[6pt]
\log\,(y+\epsilon), & \lambda=0,
\end{cases}
\label{eq:boxcox}
\end{equation}
with $\epsilon=10^{-6}$ (Box--Cox requires strictly positive input), the output
standardized to zero mean and unit variance, and $\lambda$ fit once, globally, on
the pooled pretraining load rather than per building---all exactly as in the
official implementation. Calendar features (day-of-year, day-of-week,
hour-of-day) are linearly scaled to $[-1,1]$, matching the official transform (not
sinusoidally encoded). The context length is $L=168$h and the forecast horizon is
$H=24$h. For reproducibility we note that Buildings-900K parquet rows are not
stored chronologically ($>99.9\%$ of adjacent rows are non-consecutive in time);
every window is therefore built from timestamp-sorted, monotonicity-checked series.

\subsection{Training}
\label{sec:training}
Each neural model maps the Box--Cox-transformed load history and exogenous
features to a Gaussian predictive distribution $(\mu,\sigma)$ over the 24-hour
horizon and is trained by minimizing the Gaussian negative log-likelihood,
matching the benchmark's own probabilistic head \citep{buildingsbench2023}. The
optimization recipe (Table~\ref{tab:hparams}) follows current standard practice
for deep forecasters: AdamW \citep{adamw} with a warmup--cosine learning-rate
schedule and early stopping. Every transformer baseline is sized to
$\geq7\times10^{6}$ parameters in both conditions (Table~\ref{tab:compute}) so
that weaker performance cannot be attributed to insufficient capacity, and all
models are trained under two conditions: \textbf{A} (without weather; load and
calendar only) and \textbf{B} (with weather; the seven official channels added as
exogenous inputs).

Before entering each model, the load history is normalized per window by
reversible instance normalization (RevIN, \citealp{revin}), which removes the
per-building level and scale that would otherwise dominate zero-shot transfer:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{y}_t=\frac{y_t-\mu_w}{\sigma_w},\qquad
\mu_w=\frac{1}{L}\sum_{t=1}^{L}y_t,\qquad
\sigma_w=\max\!\Big(\mathrm{std}\big(y_{1:L}\big),\,10^{-3}\Big),
\label{eq:revin}
\end{equation}
and the predicted distribution is de-normalized as
$\hat{\mu}=\mu_{\mathrm n}\,\sigma_w+\mu_w$ and
$\hat{\sigma}=\big(\mathrm{softplus}(s_{\mathrm n})+10^{-3}\big)\,\sigma_w$,
where $(\mu_{\mathrm n},s_{\mathrm n})$ are the network outputs. Following the
benchmark, validation is a temporal holdout of the final two weeks of the year on
the AMY2018-release buildings (a genuine chronological year), rather than a
random building-level split.

\subsection{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}
We report the benchmark's exact metrics \citep{buildingsbench2023}. The point
metric is NRMSE (coefficient of variation of RMSE), computed per building as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{NRMSE}_b = 100 \times
\frac{\sqrt{\frac{1}{n_b}\sum_i (\hat{y}_{b,i}-y_{b,i})^2}}
     {\frac{1}{n_b}\sum_i y_{b,i}},
\label{eq:nrmse}
\end{equation}
and aggregated as the \emph{median across buildings} within each building type,
with 95\% bootstrap confidence intervals over $2{,}000$ resamples. The
probabilistic metric (RPS) is the closed-form continuous ranked probability score
of the Gaussian forecast \citep{gneiting2007},
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{RPS}\big(\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^{2}),\,y\big)=
\sigma\left[z\big(2\Phi(z)-1\big)+2\varphi(z)-\frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}}\right],
\qquad z=\frac{y-\mu}{\sigma},
\label{eq:crps}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi$ and $\varphi$ are the standard normal distribution and density
functions; it is reported in physical kWh by passing $\mu\pm\sigma$ through the
inverse Box--Cox transform and averaging the two half-widths (the benchmark's own
Gaussian approximation).

\subsection{Real-Building Weather Acquisition}
\label{sec:weather-data}
Evaluating condition B on real buildings requires the same seven weather channels
the models were trained on, but the benchmark publishes only temperature (and a
derived humidity) for its real datasets the remaining five channels (wind
speed/direction and the three radiation components) are absent. We therefore fetch
all seven channels from the Open-Meteo ERA5 reanalysis archive
\citep{openmeteo,era5}, the same reanalysis family the benchmark authors used, at
the same locations, date ranges, and fixed local-standard-time offsets recovered
verbatim from their data-generation scripts, and in matching physical units
($^\circ$C, \%, m/s, degrees, W/m$^2$).

Because a location, timezone, or unit error would silently corrupt this input, we
gate every fetched series against ground truth before use: we require the fetched
temperature to match the benchmark's own published ERA5 temperature at Pearson
$r>0.95$ with zero best-lag (a timezone slip shifts the diurnal cycle and moves the
best-lag off zero) and a mean offset below $3^\circ$C (a unit error inflates it).
All ten source locations passed, with $r\in[0.958,0.995]$ and temperature offsets
below $1.1^\circ$C, confirming correct alignment before any real-building weather
result is computed. This acquired weather is applied only at evaluation time the
condition-B checkpoints are reused unchanged so any change in real-building error
reflects the value of the weather inputs, not additional training.

\subsection{Weather Value: In-Distribution versus Zero-Shot Transfer}
\label{sec:weather}
Weather inputs help substantially in-distribution but not under zero-shot transfer,
and this contrast is the paper's central finding. On the simulated test set
(Table~\ref{tab:weather}), adding weather reduces commercial NRMSE for every trained
model, with gains concentrated in cross-dimension and exogenous-aware attention
architectures (Crossformer $-21.3\%$, TimeXer $-10.9\%$) and only marginal change
elsewhere ($1$--$3\%$ for the linear, recurrent, and tree models). This pattern
mirrors the loss curves of Figure~\ref{fig:loss}: architectures built to model
cross-variable interactions extract more from the weather channels when the test
distribution matches training.

The same weather inputs provide no benefit on real buildings. Using the validated
seven-channel acquisition of Section~\ref{sec:weather-data}, we re-evaluate every
condition-B checkpoint on the real set and compare against its no-weather result
(Table~\ref{tab:weather-real}). The mean change is $+0.05$ commercial and $+0.64$
residential NRMSE i.e.\ flat to marginally worse no model improves on both
building types, and the largest single-model reduction is only $1.25$ NRMSE points
(iTransformer, residential). Crossformer is the decisive case: its
$-21.3\%$ in-distribution gain collapses to $+0.13\%$ ($+0.017$ NRMSE) on real
buildings, statistically indistinguishable from zero. The weather sensitivity these
models acquire on simulated ResStock/ComStock load therefore does not describe how
real metered buildings respond, so applying it zero-shot adds noise rather than
signal. A benchmark that reports only in-distribution weather ablations as the
loss curves and Table~\ref{tab:weather} alone would would report the opposite
conclusion.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
Several factors bound the scope of our conclusions. First, training uses a $20$K
subsample of Buildings-900K rather than the full $\sim$900K corpus; although the
benchmark's power-law scaling (Section~\ref{sec:dataset}) implies the residual
benefit of the remaining buildings is small, absolute numbers are still not
directly comparable to a full-corpus pretraining run, and the comparison in
Table~\ref{tab:vs-paper} is therefore not a controlled head-to-head. Second,
the probabilistic metric (RPS) uses the benchmark's inverse-Box--Cox Gaussian
approximation; relative model-to-model comparisons are valid, but absolute RPS
values inherit that approximation's error, and we observed the real-building RPS to
sit on a different scale from the simulated set, which we did not fully diagnose.
Third, the real weather is a validated but single-point ERA5 reanalysis, whereas the
training weather derives from the simulation's own meteorological files; a residual
source mismatch cannot be fully excluded even though temperature aligns to
$r>0.95$. Fourth, our zero-shot finding measures the transfer of \emph{frozen}
learned weather sensitivity and does not speak to what fine-tuning on real buildings
would recover. Finally, the per-channel knockout analysis showed two attention
models (Crossformer, TimeXer) degrading disproportionately under a zeroed channel,
a response we flag as possibly reflecting numerical sensitivity to out-of-support
inputs rather than genuine reliance, and we therefore do not draw per-channel
importance conclusions from it.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}
The simulated-to-real weather gap suggests three directions. First, transfer
learning: the original benchmark reports that fine-tuning on a modest amount of real
data helps, and re-measuring weather value \emph{after} fine-tuning would separate
``weather is uninformative for real STLF'' from ``sim-trained sensitivity does not
transfer zero-shot.'' Second, distribution alignment: matching the statistics of
real ERA5 weather to the simulation's weather distribution, or training with weather
augmentation, may make learned sensitivity more transportable. Third, coverage:
completing the tree-model and original Transformer-\{S,M,L\} checkpoints under our
identical pipeline would turn Table~\ref{tab:vs-paper} into a controlled comparison,
and extending the analysis to longer horizons and extreme-weather subsets would test
whether weather value concentrates in operating conditions that annual medians
average away.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We re-benchmarked a broad set of canonical deep forecasting architectures on
BuildingsBench under its exact preprocessing and evaluation protocol, with each
transformer sized to a common parameter budget. Three findings stand out. First,
the re-implementation is faithful closed-form persistence baselines reproduce the
benchmark's published values to within $0.5$ NRMSE points, and every trained model
outperforms every persistence baseline on the simulated
set. Second, attention-based transformers (Crossformer, TimeXer, PatchTST)
form a clearly separated top tier, with rankings that shift across settings:
Crossformer wins the simulated set with weather, while TimeXer generalizes best to
real buildings zero-shot, competitive with and on our two aggregates ahead of the
benchmark's largest pretrained transformer. Third, and most consequential, the
large in-distribution gains from weather ($-21\%$ NRMSE for Crossformer on simulated
data) do not transfer to real zero-shot buildings, where the same validated
seven-channel weather leaves error essentially unchanged (mean commercial NRMSE
change $+0.05$). Weather sensitivity learned on simulated load is thus an
in-distribution property, not a transportable one a distinction that only a
benchmark evaluating weather under its own zero-shot shift can reveal.
